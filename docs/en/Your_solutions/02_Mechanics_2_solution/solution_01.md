# Physics Solution: Gravitational Dependence of a Simple Pendulum

This document analyzes how gravitational acceleration affects the period of a simple pendulum and calculates the specific length required for a "seconds pendulum" on Earth.

---

## 1. Problem Statement

A simple pendulum has a **period of 4 seconds** on Earth.
1.  **Moon Calculation:** What would its period be on the Moon, where $g_{moon} \approx \frac{1}{6}g_{earth}$?
2.  **Length Calculation:** What is the required length ($L$) of a simple pendulum to have a period of exactly **1 second** on Earth?

---

## 2. Fundamental Formula

The period ($T$) of a simple pendulum for small angles is given by:

$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

Where:
* $T$: Period (seconds)
* $L$: Length of the pendulum (meters)
* $g$: Acceleration due to gravity ($\approx 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2$ on Earth)

---

## 3. Part A: Period on the Moon

### Mathematical Relationship
Since $2\pi$ and $L$ are constant, we can see that the period is inversely proportional to the square root of gravity:
$$T \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}$$

We can set up a ratio between Earth ($E$) and Moon ($M$):
$$\frac{T_M}{T_E} = \frac{\sqrt{g_E}}{\sqrt{g_M}}$$

### Calculation
Given that $g_M = \frac{1}{6}g_E$:
$$\frac{T_M}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{g_E}}{\sqrt{g_E / 6}} = \sqrt{6}$$

$$T_M = 4 \cdot \sqrt{6}$$
$$T_M \approx 4 \cdot 2.449 \approx 9.80 \, \text{seconds}$$

**Result:** The period on the Moon would be approximately **9.80 seconds**.
